2018 Mercedes-Benz E400 Coupe review: Supple stunner

by Ben Stewart

2018 Mercedes-Benz E400 Coupe review: Supple stunner

At a time when its rivals are trimming back on their two-door lineups, Mercedes-Benz is doubling down on coupes. The automaker has more two-door models than any other luxury brand. “We pride ourselves on having the broadest product offering in the luxury market. That’s true for the core segments, but I think that’s true for the relevant niches like coupes, too,” says Bernie Glaser, general manager of product management. “And the coupe market is an attractive one because the buyers are loyal. They don’t buy SUVs -- they want their stylish coupe.” Yes, Mercedes-Benz is clearly a coupe specialist, and much of that comes from a long tradition of crafting some of the world’s most comfortable, chic and stylish personal luxury machines. Look no further than the current S-Class Coupe. It’s stunning. And as it turns out, those good genes have been passed down to its younger brother, the new seventh-generation E400 coupe.

Throttle-Back Thursday: The grey market Citroen CX packed ‘the impact of Grace Jones riding a hovercraft down Maple Street’
by Graham Kozak

Throttle-Back Thursday

Technically, this Citroen CX imported for sale to the United States came to our shores from the Netherlands, not France. Technically, it’s not a Citroen CX at all -- it’s a CX Automotive Prestige, brought to America and sold by CX Automotive’s Malcolm Langman, but only after the spacey French cruiser underwent a substantial crash safety-improving overhaul and refit in a Dutch garage. Orchestrating a grey market import might have been possible in the 1980s, but it sure wasn’t easy to federalize a car with no U.S.-market analog. Is it worth it? If you’re a French auto enthusiast, absolutely, even with its whopper of a $38,400 price tag. Shorter than a Chevrolet Monte Carlo, but with a longer wheelbase than a Cadillac Fleetwood, it was designed and built under the constraints of an automotive logic wholly different from anything fans of America, British, German or even Japanese cars would have been familiar with. In short, it's weird. As weird, we wrote, as “Grace Jones riding a hovercraft down Maple Street.” Even if its cabin wasn’t quite Mercedes-Benz spec in terms of luxury appointments, it was arguably the most distinctive car in its class (assuming it was even part of a class). It was, for all intents and purposes, a four-door exotic with unparalleled ride comfort. We're not surprised the American market didn't gobble them up by the thousands, but if this sort of car appeals to you, good news: Built by Citroen from 1974-1991, every example of this model is now legal to import as a classic -- no Dutch refit required.

How Dodge plans to fight Challenger SRT Demon dealer markups

BY LARRY P. VELLEQUETTE AT AUTOMOTIVE NEWS

How Dodge plans to fight Challenger SRT Demon dealer markups

Remember the very public mess that Dodge encountered with some of its dealers when the Hellcat debuted in 2014? It's not going to go through that again when ordering opens up Wednesday for the 840-hp Challenger SRT Demon. Dodge said Tuesday that the allocation method for a limited edition of 3,000 Demons in the U.S. and 300 in Canada will give priority to vehicles sold at or below the Demon's $86,090 sticker price, including delivery and gas guzzler tax. Demons sold above sticker will be produced only after the "priority production" is completed. That will put pressure on dealers not to place immediate profit-rich markups on at least the first run of Demons, due to arrive in dealerships this fall.

Study: Koreans win in initial quality; U.S. is ahead of Europe and Japan

BY ROBIN WARNER

Study: Koreans win in initial quality; U.S. is ahead of Europe and Japan

J.D. Power announced Kia has the highest initial quality for the second year in a row. New Korean luxury brand Genesis finishes second while the house brand, Hyundai, came in sixth. The top domestic brand, Ford, finished fourth -- a huge improvement from last year, in no small part thanks to improved functionality of the Sync 3 infotainment system. Porsche led the Europeans in third place; BMW also did well, tying with Chevrolet and Hyundai in sixth. That’s essentially no change from their fifth-place finish last year. The top-placed Japanese maker is Nissan, ranking 10th. Toyota just barely beat the industry average in 13th spot. This is a good time to give a bit of context: The auto industry as a whole improved by nearly 8 percent from last year, and all cars have vastly improved quality. Some makes improved and others dropped on the list based on how J.D. Power conducts the study. The firm looks at two types of issues that ding initial quality: defects -- things that don’t work well, or at all -- and design issues, things that work as built, but weren’t designed in a way that customers enjoy. Generally speaking, the Japanese cars have very few defects and lose most points because of design, which means the “problem” is debatable. Alas, the rules are the same for everyone, resulting in both Japan and Europe falling behind the U.S. and Korea.
